THE STATE OF WORLD HAPPINESS (2021)

WORLDWIDE HAPPINESS

The happiness scores are obtained from the World Happiness Report (2021), which is a survey conducted by the Sustainable Development Solutions Network to undertand the state of global happiness.

The world map displayed below visualises the happiness scores, also known as ladder scores, of 149 countries.

Click on the filter below to view the happiness scores by region

REGIONAL HAPPINESS

Generally, different regions in the world have varrying happiness scores.

We observe that countries in the North American and NZ region generally have happiness scores starting from 7 onwards.

On the contrary, countries in the Sub-Saharan Africa region recorded happiness scores mostly ranging between 4 and 5. This could perhaps be due to the under-development or lack of infrastructure within the region.

TOP 5 HAPPIEST AND SADDEST COUNTRIES

The diagram to the left shows the top 5 happiest and saddest countries.

Finland tops the table with a happiness score of 7.8 while Afghanistan sits at the bottom with a score of 2.5.

Interestingly, all top 5 countries in this list are located in Western Europe while 4 of the bottom 5 countries are from the Sub-Saharan African region.

Select any region listed on the legend to filter the countries by region.

HAPPINESS OVER THE YEARS

The rankings of each country based on their respective happiness scores continuously changes over the years.

From 2016 to 2020, Finland consistently dominated top spot, averaging a happiness score of at least 7.6.

Toggle the slider at the bottom of the visualisation to compare between the top 5 to top 10 ranked countries.

Select a country on the legend to highlight its progression in rankings.

FACTORS AFFECTING HAPPINESS

To understand what makes a country happy, we need to look at the factors affecting happiness.

With that, the happiness score of each country is calculated based on 6 key measures: Log GDP per Capita, Social Support, Health Life Expectancy, Freedom to Make Life Choices, Generosity, Perceptions of Corruption and Dystopia & Residual.
